About Saudi German Hospitals
Saudi German Hospitals (SGH) is one of the largest private healthcare networks in the Middle East and Africa. Founded in 1988, the group operates a growing portfolio of hospitals, medical centres, and specialist clinics across Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Egypt, and Yemen. The network is committed to delivering international-standard healthcare through CBAHI and JCI accreditation, technology-driven operations, and a patient-centred care model.

Census KPIs, Measures & Formulas
A plain-language reference for every term, indicator and formula used in this Census Analysis. Each entry gives the term as used in the dashboard, what it means, how it is calculated, and a worked example.
One worked example used throughout: Aseer, January 2025 — Active Beds = 297 · Inpatient Days = 6,134.7 · Admissions = 1,265 · Discharges = 1,287 · Days in month = 31. Following this single hospital-month through every formula below makes the relationships easy to see.
Active Beds
Available / Licensed Beds
The number of staffed, operational beds available to admit patients during the period. It is the denominator for occupancy and turnover. In this dataset it is fixed per branch (the licensed/operational capacity), e.g. Aseer 297, Riyadh 279, Madinah 203, Jeddah 195, Makkah 173, Hail 127, HAJ 34 — a network total of 1,308 beds.
Source
Active Beds = staffed beds available for inpatient admission
Example: Aseer operates 297 active beds — the capacity against which its occupancy and turnover are measured.
Admissions
ADM
The count of patients formally admitted to an inpatient bed during the period. It measures inflow of demand. Admissions are counted when a patient enters — regardless of whether their stay is complete.
Formula
Admissions = total inpatient admissions in the period
Example: Aseer admitted 1,265 patients in January 2025.
Discharges
Separations
The count of patients whose inpatient episode ended during the period — sent home, transferred out, or deceased. In hospital statistics this is also called “separations.” Because a discharge marks a completed stay, it is the correct denominator for length-of-stay and turnover (you can only measure how long a stay lasted once it has finished).
Formula
Discharges = home discharges + transfers out + deaths
Example: Aseer recorded 1,287 discharges in January 2025.
Inpatient Days
IPD / Patient-Days
The total number of days all patients spent occupying a bed during the period — the sum of every patient’s daily bed-count. It is the core volume measure of bed usage and feeds directly into Average Daily Census and Bed Occupancy Rate. One patient staying 5 days contributes 5 inpatient days; ten patients each staying 1 day also contribute 10.
Formula
Inpatient Days = sum of occupied beds across every day in the period
Example: Aseer accumulated 6,134.7 inpatient days across the 31 days of January 2025.
Average Daily Census
ADC
The average number of beds occupied on any given day during the period. It converts a period’s total inpatient days into a single “typical day” figure, and effectively equals the average number of occupied beds.
Formula
ADC = Inpatient Days ÷ Days in Period
Example: Aseer, Jan 2025  =  6,134.7 ÷ 31  =  197.9 patients per day (so on a typical January day, ~198 of Aseer’s 297 beds were occupied).
Occupied Beds vs Available Beds
Capacity gap
A capacity comparison: how many beds are in use (on average) versus how many exist. Average occupied beds equals the ADC; the difference is unused (idle) capacity. This is the basis of the “Available vs Occupied” chart in the Overview tab.
Formula
Occupied Beds (avg) = ADC  |  Idle Beds = Active Beds − ADC
Example: Aseer, Jan 2025  =  297 available − 197.9 occupied  =  ~99 idle beds on a typical day.
Bed Occupancy Rate
BOR
The percentage of available bed capacity that was actually used during the period — the headline efficiency indicator. Low BOR signals under-utilised capacity; very high or above-100% BOR signals overflow admissions, beds turned over more than once a day, or census-timing effects (legitimate, but worth verifying).
Formula
BOR = ADC ÷ Active Beds × 100
     = Inpatient Days ÷ (Active Beds × Days in Period) × 100
Example: Aseer, Jan 2025  =  197.9 ÷ 297 × 100  =  66.6%.
Reference band: 75–85% is widely cited as the “optimal” acute-care range. See the sourcing note at the foot of this page — this band comes from OECD / academic literature, not an official Saudi MOH mandate.
Average Length of Stay
ALOS
The average number of days a patient stays per completed inpatient episode. It reflects case mix and care efficiency: shorter stays free beds faster, but stays that are too short can signal premature discharge. The denominator is discharges, not admissions, because length of stay can only be measured once a stay is complete (a still-admitted patient’s stay is not yet final).
Formula
ALOS = Inpatient Days ÷ Discharges
Example: Aseer, Jan 2025  =  6,134.7 ÷ 1,287  =  4.77 days per patient.
Reference band: ~3.5–4.5 days for general acute medical/surgical care, but ALOS is highly case-mix dependent — high-turnover branches like HAJ legitimately run ~2.5–2.8 days.
Bed Turnover Rate
BTR
How many patients each bed serves during the period — a throughput/productivity measure. High BTR means beds are being recycled quickly (efficient throughput or short stays); low BTR means slower throughput. BTR, BOR and ALOS move together: for a given occupancy, shorter stays produce higher turnover.
Formula
BTR = Discharges ÷ Active Beds  (per period)
Example: Aseer, Jan 2025  =  1,287 ÷ 297  =  4.33 turns — each Aseer bed served about 4.3 patients that month.
Note: A frequently-cited general-ward benchmark is ≈4–5 turns/month, but this is unsuitable for small high-throughput units like HAJ, where ~9–12 turns/month is normal.
Days in Period
Helper
The number of calendar days in the period being measured — used to convert inpatient days into a daily average (ADC) and to compute bed-capacity-days for BOR. This analysis uses exact calendar days per month, including 29 days for February 2024 (leap year), so monthly occupancy is not distorted by month length.
Values
Month = 28–31 days  |  Bed-capacity-days = Active Beds × Days in Period
Example: January 2025 has 31 days, so Aseer’s capacity was 297 × 31 = 9,207 bed-days that month.

Sourcing note. There is no official Saudi MOH-mandated BOR target. The 75–85% band derives from OECD acute-care averages and academic literature and is best labelled “OECD / academic consensus.” The Saudi MOH publishes actual occupancy figures through its Statistical Yearbook but does not mandate a specific range. All reference bands above are indicative guides for interpretation, not regulatory thresholds, and should be read alongside each branch’s case mix and role.

Methodology note — ALOS & BTR denominator. This analysis calculates ALOS and BTR on discharges (completed inpatient episodes), since length of stay and turnover can only be measured once a stay has finished. This follows the AHIMA and WHO-Europe convention. Note that OECD and CDC/NCHS define these measures on admissions or discharges interchangeably, and a common bed-turnover definition uses admissions ÷ beds. Because admissions and discharges differ month to month, the discharge basis yields slightly different values than an admission basis — the discharge convention is applied consistently throughout this report.
References & Benchmark Sourcing
Evidence base for the occupancy, length-of-stay and turnover benchmarks applied in the SGH KSA Census Analysis
What the evidence converges on
  • Acute-care Bed Occupancy Rate is widely benchmarked at ~80–85%, with 85% the most commonly cited ceiling beyond which bed shortages, ED delays and hospital-acquired infection risk rise.
  • The 80–90% “efficient” band originates in health-economics literature, not a single national regulator.
  • Saudi actuals for major medical cities sit in the high-70s to ~80% range — consistent with, but not mandated by, the international band.
  • BOR read in isolation can mislead; the Pabón-Lasso model pairs BOR with Bed Turnover Rate and ALOS to classify true efficiency.
